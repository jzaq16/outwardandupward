Modern Apostles and Prophets: Living Proof of God's Love

Prophets? Now?

It’s an incredible idea, isn’t it? The concept of living prophets in Christianity today.

If you think this sounds crazy, I don’t blame you. My first instinct is to question and take things apart, so it took a lot of personal experience and time for me to trust the evidence on living prophets. Now I am incredibly grateful for this gift. For me, the idea that God speaks to humanity today in the same way that He did through Moses, Isaiah, Paul and others is a miraculous demonstration of His love. Practically, it's also a source of peace and clarity.

I'm writing about this because of recent, important events. On October 14, 2025, Dallin H. Oaks became the 18th President of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. This happened after President Russell M. Nelson passed away at 101 years old. Within my faith, both of these men are representatives of God and Christ as fully as any other prophet or apostle ever mentioned in the Bible.

I want to explain what this means for me, especially for anyone who might not be familiar with this idea.

Jesus Christ is the Foundation

I have to point out something critical first:

Prophets point to Christ. That’s their mission. It’s what they do (Acts 10:43). In fact, the apostle John recorded in the Book of Revelation that "the testimony of Jesus is the spirit of prophecy" (Revelation 19:10, emphasis added). Another ancient prophet in the Book of Mormon wrote:

“And we talk of Christ⁠, we rejoice in Christ⁠, we preach of Christ⁠, we prophesy of Christ⁠, and we write according to our prophecies, that our children may know to what source they may look for a remission of their sins” (2 Nephi 25:26).

Prophets teach us to know, worship, follow, and love Christ.

An Ancient Pattern Continues Today

I don’t believe in a God who sees us as a science experiment, a cool hobby to show off in front of His friends, or a house pet. He is our Father. He wants to talk with us and be a part of our life! He wants to talk to you (1) personally (lots more on that to come), and also (2) by calling prophets. The Biblical prophet Amos wrote: “Surely the Lord God will do nothing, but he revealeth his secret unto his servants the prophets” (Amos 3:7). He’s done this from the beginning, pausing only in times when we've not been ready to listen.

Similarly, when Jesus was on the earth, He set up His church on a “foundation of apostles and prophets” (Ephesians 4:11-13). He chose twelve apostles, led by Peter (Matthew 10:1-8, Matthew 16:15-19), and gave them the authority to lead His church.

This ancient pattern continues as today’s template. The president of the church is the senior apostle (like the apostle Peter), and there are also twelve apostles called to be prophets, with a special mission to testify of Jesus Christ and share God’s messages. Just like Peter, James, John, and Paul in the New Testament, apostles and prophets today testify of Jesus Christ and share God’s perspectives on what is taking place right now.

Wait… This raises a whole lot of questions.

Definitely! And these deserve more thorough answers than can be recorded here, but I can try to rapid-fire a few.

  1. Can’t we just personally pray and talk to God? Are prophets really necessary?
    Yes! God wants to reveal things to you personally. If you pray, listen, and act on what you learn through the Holy Spirit your life will change (again, tons to discuss later). Still, God also has a lot to say that applies to all of us. This allows Him to help us accomplish more together, reach us when we’re not very receptive to God, and give us a clear path to more of His will and perspective. Prophets make this possible, and it’s a tremendous gift!
  2. But can anyone just call themselves a prophet? How does this work?
    I suppose I could grab a megaphone and a camera crew and call myself a modern prophet, but I wouldn’t be one – even if I somehow convinced millions of people that I were. Only God, through Christ and those called by Christ can call another apostle or prophet. This is exactly why Christ set up a church and gave His apostles authority – to baptize, to act in power, to give the Holy Ghost, and also to call new apostles (Matthew 10, Acts 8:17-20,1 Timothy 4:14, Acts 1:15-26).1
    The original 12 apostles were personally called by Christ. Because of the holy responsibility they had from God, they were authorized to receive revelation to call new apostles when there was a vacancy. This principle is the same today. There are apostles called today, and it turns out, all of them are ordained as prophets. They testify of Christ, receive revelation from God in that important role, and they council together about important matters. The most senior apostle, however, is a prophet with a special responsibility to receive revelation for the entire church of Christ: a prophet with capital “P” so to speak. This senior apostle is always the longest tenured apostle, so there is no voting or question about who is the next prophet when a current prophet dies. The person who has been an apostle for the longest period of time will be the next prophet. So who really chooses the next prophet? God does, because he controls the timing of passing into the next life.3
  3. But wait, what’s been going on for the last 2000 or so years?
    The gospel of Jesus Christ has spread marvelously, and countless men and women made sacrifices to accomplish this. That said, most of this happened without apostles or a living prophet. The original apostles were killed in time, and people commonly disagreed about many points of belief. Because conditions weren’t right, God waited to call another prophet and set of apostles. When the time and place was right, God called Joseph Smith in 1820 and again established Christ's church as the Church of Jesus Christ - the same one as when He was on the earth, but now in the last or "latter" days. This brought back the responsibility and authorization to receive direction for the world as a whole and act using the power that Christ gave to prophets and apostles throughout history. There have been authorized successors to the apostles and prophets since that time, and Christ has said that this would continue until He comes again (D&C 13:1).

So what are prophets actually saying in modern times?

A lot! Like ancient prophets and apostles, they often help us apply what God and Christ have already said - but adapted to the world as it is today. This very often is a call to change or repent. Sometimes they reveal new things that Christ would have us do because it will bless us. Sometimes they warn about things that are coming.

Practically, where do I access the words of modern prophets?

While prophets and apostles travel around the world to share messages under specific circumstances, twice a year there is a global event where the prophet, apostles, and many others called to special positions within the Church of Jesus Christ address a global audience. These sermons can be found in the General Conference section of the online Gospel Library of the Church of Jesus Christ, or in its mobile app, on iOS or Android. Note that there any many other things in the Gospel Library, including the scriptures and other resources.

I and many others actively study these sermons and treat them as messages from servants of God. I look at these just as I study words written by prophets and apostles in the Old Testament, New Testament, Book of Mormon and other scriptures, except that these are given with today's circumstances in mind.

Some Parting Thoughts

As I've discussed, the fact that God calls prophets today is ultimately a sign that He loves us. He is deeply invested in our happiness. He wants to be a part of our lives. Your life, too! It is a remarkable blessing that might sound fantastic and mind-blowing to some, but it can also be so natural that I and others actually risk taking this miracle for granted.

Returning again to the earlier idea – prophets point to Christ; they share God’s perspective for us in our day; their very existence is mighty evidence of God’s love and concern for all of His children. That said, as extraordinary and essential as the prophets' role is, they would be the first to tell you that they are not to be worshiped or mindlessly followed. Just as Christ always pointed to His Father (John 8:26-29) prophets always point to Christ. As we pay attention to Christ's representatives, It is a personal responsibility to seek our own relationship with God and gain a personal knowledge of Christ’s teachings. Our goal is to develop a deep connection with God individually so His spirit can confirm to us truth as we seek it out from prophets, scripture, personal revelation, other sources of truth of all kinds, and our own desires to do good. Knowledge gained in this way will stay with us and shape who we are and what we do, because it is put in our hearts by God. When all is said and done, our journey of faith is ultimately ours to make.
